History of Warren

Historian – Garry Aney · mobile: (315) 219-9797 · email: garryaney@aol.com

In Memory of the Brave Patriots who suffered and died in the massacre of Andrustown on July 18, 1778. This tablet is erected by the descendants of the first settlers: Bell, Crim, Frank, Hoyer, Lepper, Osterhout, and Stauring (Daughters of the American Revolution).

From the Gazetteer and Business Directory of Herkimer County, NY: Warren, named in honor of General Joseph Warren, was formed February 5, 1796 (Columbia separated in 1812). Jordanville grew around churches, stores, and mechanic shops, while Little Lakes hosted churches, hotels, and the Wall sawmill producing about 100,000 feet annually with fifty rakes made for retail.

The Warren cheese factory produced 90,000 pounds annually, and Daniel Tilden’s factory at Warren Center reached 100,000 pounds. Wertherbee's Mills, in the south-west, ground 50,000 bushels yearly and cut 200,000 feet of lumber.

Mrs. H.D. Cruger’s 1,500-acre Henderson Home estate featured a mansion and thirteen tenant houses. Settlements began before the Revolution by Germans from the upper valley at Andrustown and Little Lakes. Andrustown was burned by Brant in 1778 with sieges and captives; Youngs Settlement was spared for Tory reasons but later plundered by Americans.

In 1792 Samuel Cleland from Massachusetts and his five sons became the first New England family to settle here alongside other early families. Isaac Freeman built the first grist mill in 1795, and Elder Phineas Holcomb was the first minister around 1793. Baptist, Methodist, and Reformed churches formed between 1799 and 1854.

The 1865 population reached 1,611 across 23,405 acres. These stories and the town’s resilience continue to guide Heritage programming and research.
